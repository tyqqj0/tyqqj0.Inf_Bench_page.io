Abstract

Humans naturally possess the spatial reasoning ability to form and manipulate images and structures of objects in space. There is an increasing effort to endow Vision-Language Models (VLMs) with similar spatial reasoning capabilities. However, it remains unclear whether these models truly understand and manipulate spatial objects or rely on simpler pathways for solving tasks. In this work, we introduce Inf-Bench, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate spatial reasoning in VLMs. Specifically, we focus on spatial deformation reasoning—a key aspect of spatial intelligence that requires understanding how objects transform through spatial operations. Our benchmark presents an infinite ladder of complexity through three levels of spatial worlds (2D, 2.5D, and 3D) and two types of reasoning (forward and inverse). This design creates a systematic framework to push and evaluate the limits of VLMs' spatial reasoning abilities. Through extensive evaluation, we demonstrate that current state-of-the-art VLMs struggle with spatial deformation reasoning, especially as complexity increases along our infinite ladder. Our findings reveal specific weaknesses and limitations in current models, providing valuable insights for future research directions in spatial reasoning for vision-language intelligence.

Why Inf-Bench? (Motivation & Introduction)

Imagine shaping a lump of clay into a desired object through sequential manipulations, considering how the object's appearance changes with each action. This process exemplifies spatial deformation reasoning—the ability to imagine, predict, and reason about how objects transform through spatial operations.

The Need for Spatial Reasoning Evaluation

While Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities across various tasks, their ability to reason about spatial transformations remains largely untested. Current benchmarks typically focus on basic spatial concepts but rarely examine complex spatial operations and transformations, especially in a systematic way that increases in complexity.

Existing benchmarks either focus on static spatial relationships (like object positions) or simple operations, without a cohesive framework to systematically test increasingly complex spatial reasoning. This gap motivates our development of Inf-Bench.

The Infinite Ladder Concept

Inf-Bench introduces the concept of an "infinite ladder" of spatial reasoning complexity. This ladder has two dimensions:

  • Spatial Worlds: Progressing from 2D shapes to 2.5D layered structures to 3D objects (Rubik's cubes)
  • Reasoning Types: Both forward reasoning (predicting results from operations) and inverse reasoning (inferring operations from results)

As one ascends this ladder, tasks become increasingly complex, requiring more sophisticated spatial understanding and reasoning capabilities.

Benchmark Overview

Inf-Bench evaluates spatial deformation reasoning across three spatial worlds, each representing a different level of complexity in spatial structures and operations.

2D World

Inspired by the game Shapez.io, our 2D world involves basic geometric shapes that undergo operations like cutting, rotation, coloring, and more.

Operations:

  • Cut, Mirror, Rotate
  • Stack, Colorize, Add Window

These primitive operations can be combined to create complex transformations, testing models' understanding of 2D spatial changes.

2.5D World

Our 2.5D world introduces layered structures, where 2D shapes are stacked to create more complex objects with depth.

Operations:

  • Layer-wise 2D operations
  • Layer swapping, merging, splitting
  • Multi-layer transformations

This intermediate level bridges the gap between 2D and full 3D reasoning, requiring understanding of how changes in one layer affect the overall structure.

3D World

At the highest level, our 3D world uses Rubik's cubes as a testbed for complex spatial reasoning, requiring understanding of 3D rotations and their effects.

Operations:

  • Standard Rubik's cube notation (F, B, R, L, U, D)
  • Extended operations (slice moves like M, E, S)
  • Compound moves and sequences

This level challenges models to perform genuine 3D spatial reasoning, imagining how cube faces move and change with operations.

Reasoning Types

Forward Reasoning

Given an initial state and a sequence of operations, predict the final state.

Input: Initial shape/structure + Operations sequence
Output: Resulting shape/structure

This tests a model's ability to mentally simulate spatial operations and their cumulative effects.

Inverse Reasoning

Given an initial state and a final state, infer the operations that were performed.

Input: Initial shape/structure + Final shape/structure
Output: Operations sequence that transforms initial to final

This tests a model's ability to reverse-engineer spatial transformations, a more challenging task that requires deeper understanding.

The Inf-Bench dataset is publicly available for research purposes. It includes task examples across all three spatial worlds and both reasoning types, along with ground truth annotations.

Data Format

The dataset is provided in JSON format with the following structure:

{
  "id": "task_id",
  "world": "2D|2.5D|3D",
  "reasoning_type": "forward|inverse",
  "difficulty": "easy|medium|hard",
  "initial_state": {...},
  "operations": [...],  // for forward reasoning
  "final_state": {...},
  "options": [...],     // for multiple choice questions
  "correct_answer": "..."
}
